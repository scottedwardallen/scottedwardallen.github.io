## Research Overview

His research shows that neural information regularities are key to understanding fundamental learning mechanisms, that these mechanisms drive the development of trust, and that building trust enables effective organizational change.

His work addresses several critical gaps. First, human learning underlies many important behaviors, yet prevailing models remain largely descriptive rather than mechanistic. Second, trust is central to many societal and organizational outcomes, but most research remains correlational and rarely establishes causal pathways for building trust in practice. Third, while processes for planning and implementing organizational change are well understood, the frameworks used to communicate them are often so complex that they are difficult to apply in real-world settings.

Dr. Allen’s research closes the first gap by modeling the types of information encoded and used in neural systems, enabling mechanistic accounts of learning dynamics and error modes. This approach generates concrete, testable predictions for learning and decision-making, including the formation of trust.

His work closes the second gap through field experiments using novel methodologies to establish causal evidence for strategies that build trust.

It closes the third gap by translating implementation science into streamlined, practitioner-focused principles and protocols that support effective change in real organizations.

His work bridges phenomena across levels of neurophysics, cognition, behavior, and institutions.
